# First-Principles Stock Investigation Checklist

Use this checklist as an evidence map. It is acceptable to mark items as `Unknown`; it is not acceptable to silently assume them.

## Identity

- Ticker, exchange, currency, share class, company legal name.
- Security type: common stock, ADR, H-share, A-share, preferred, fund, or other claim.
- Market cap, enterprise value, share count, float, and recent dilution.
- Analysis date and market data timestamp.

## Source Ledger

Prefer current primary sources:

- Company investor relations page.
- Annual report and latest quarterly/interim report.
- Earnings release, transcript, or presentation.
- SEC EDGAR for U.S. issuers and ADRs.
- HKEXnews for Hong Kong issuers.
- CNINFO and official exchange disclosures for mainland China issuers.
- Official regulator, court, antitrust, industry, or macro sources when they affect the thesis.

Use secondary sources only for quote snapshots, consensus estimates, or news cross-checks. Label them as secondary.

## Business Reality

- Customer job: what problem is solved and why payment happens.
- Buyer, user, payer, and channel. Identify who has bargaining power.
- Revenue model: price, volume, take rate, subscription, transaction, advertising, licensing, commodity spread, or other driver.
- Demand driver: replacement cycle, installed base, regulation, income growth, habit, productivity gain, scarcity, or speculation.
- Industry structure: number of competitors, substitutes, supplier power, customer concentration, regulation, and barriers to entry.

## Economics

- Gross margin and why it exists.
- Operating margin and fixed-cost leverage.
- Return on invested capital or a close proxy.
- Capital intensity: capex, R&D, inventory, receivables, prepayments, leases, and maintenance needs.
- Working-capital pattern and whether growth consumes or releases cash.
- Cash conversion: net income vs operating cash flow vs free cash flow.
- Stock-based compensation, share issuance, buybacks, and true per-share economics.
- Segment revenue, segment margin, backlog, book-to-bill, remaining performance obligations, or deferred revenue where relevant.
- Customer concentration and whether revenue quality depends on one buyer, one contract, or one government budget.
- Cyclicality and operating leverage under stress.

## Moat And Durability

- What protects economics: brand, habit, switching cost, network effect, cost advantage, scale, data, patents, licenses, distribution, culture, or location.
- Why competitors cannot replicate it quickly.
- Whether the moat strengthens, stays flat, or decays as the company grows.
- Evidence of pricing power or the lack of it.
- Customer retention, churn, repeat purchase, market share, or cohort evidence where available.

## Competition

- Direct competitors and closest substitutes.
- Incumbent suppliers or platforms the customer already trusts.
- Customer procurement criteria: price, reliability, safety, switching cost, certification, integration effort, service level, or political/regulatory preference.
- Price/value wedge: why the company wins despite cost, risk, or incumbent resistance.
- Competitor attack path: undercut pricing, bundle, copy features, vertical integration, lobbying, exclusive contracts, or talent acquisition.

## Management And Capital Allocation

- Insider ownership and incentive structure.
- Track record of reinvestment, M&A, buybacks, dividends, debt issuance, and share issuance.
- Related-party transactions, pledges, governance red flags, auditor changes, or regulatory penalties.
- Whether management talks in per-share value, return on capital, and cash terms.

## Valuation

- Current market cap and enterprise value.
- Net cash/debt, maturity profile, interest burden, off-balance-sheet obligations.
- Normalized earnings, owner earnings, free cash flow, or asset value as appropriate.
- Scenario ranges: downside, base, upside.
- For each scenario: revenue, gross margin, FCF margin, dilution/share count, terminal valuation method, implied annualized return or valuation gap.
- Base rates for the industry and business model.
- What must be true for today's price to make sense.
- Margin of safety: valuation gap, balance-sheet cushion, durability, and evidence quality.

## Critical Option Or Catalyst

Use when one uncertain event dominates valuation:

- Option or catalyst: product launch, regulatory approval, new factory, major contract, turnaround, acquisition, litigation, debt refinancing, commodity cycle, or technology milestone.
- Technical feasibility and execution evidence.
- Customer demand evidence: orders, backlog, pilots, retention, usage, or budget line items.
- Unit economics if it works.
- Capital required before payoff.
- Timing and delay risk.
- Competitive response and substitute risk.
- What evidence would raise or lower probability.

## Inversion And Failure Modes

- How could the thesis be wrong?
- What could permanently impair capital?
- Which accounting numbers are easiest to manipulate?
- What hidden leverage, customer concentration, supplier dependency, or refinancing risk exists?
- What regulatory, geopolitical, technology, fraud, governance, or cycle risk could dominate the thesis?
- What evidence would force a downgrade or exit?

## Holder Lens

Use when the user already owns the stock:

- Cost basis and position size if provided.
- Original thesis vs current evidence.
- Whether new facts change intrinsic value or only market price.
- Rebalancing risk, concentration risk, liquidity, and tax caveats if relevant.
- Conditional action buckets: research further, watch, hold under stated assumptions, trim risk, exit thesis break, or wait for specific evidence.

## Final Classification

Choose a plain label and explain it:

- `High-quality but expensive`
- `Potentially mispriced`
- `Cheap for a reason`
- `Turnaround, evidence incomplete`
- `Cyclical timing problem`
- `Balance-sheet risk dominates`
- `Governance risk dominates`
- `Too hard`
- `Watchlist only`
- `Investigate further`
